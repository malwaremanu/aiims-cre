    TOPIC 1: FREUD'S PSYCHOSEXUAL DEVELOPMENT THEORY

    Simple Explanation

    Freud believed that personality develops through 5 stages from birth to adulthood. At each stage, a different body part (erogenous zone) becomes the center of pleasure and conflict. If a person gets too much or too little gratification at a stage, they become "fixated" and develop specific personality traits.

    The 5 Stages:

    1. ORAL STAGE (0-18 months)
    2. Focus: Mouth (eating, sucking, chewing)
    3. Task: Learn to trust caregivers

    4. Fixation Result: Oral-aggressive (aggressive, argumentative) or Oral-dependent (needy, dependent)

    5. ANAL STAGE (18 months - 3 years)

    6. Focus: Bowel/bladder control (toilet training)
    7. Task: Develop control and independence

    8. Fixation Result: Anal-retentive (perfectionist, orderly, stubborn) or Anal-expulsive (messy, rebellious)

    9. PHALLIC STAGE (3-6 years)

    10. Focus: Genitals, self-discovery
    11. Major Conflict: Oedipus complex (boys) / Electra complex (girls)
    12. Oedipus: Boy wants mother, competes with father, resolves by identifying with father
    13. Electra: Girl wants father, competes with mother, resolves by identifying with mother

    14. Penis Envy: Freud believed girls feel inferior for lacking penis

    15. LATENCY STAGE (6 years - puberty)

    16. Focus: Dormant sexual period
    17. Task: Develop skills, friendships, school learning

    18. Sexual energy sublimated into productive activities

    19. GENITAL STAGE (Puberty - adulthood)

    20. Focus: Mature sexual relationships
    21. Goal: Balance needs with adult responsibilities
    22. Healthy outcome: Mature, capable of love and work

    Most Asked Questions Pattern

    • Which stage involves toilet training? (Anal Stage)
    • Oedipus complex occurs in which stage? (Phallic Stage)
    • What is latency period? (Sexual dormancy period)
    • Anal fixation leads to which traits? (Perfectionism, orderliness, stubbornness)
    • Who proposed psychosexual development? (Sigmund Freud)

    Key Terms to Remember

    • Libido: Sexual/life energy that motivates behavior
    • Erogenous Zone: Body parts sensitive to pleasure
    • Fixation: Getting stuck at a stage due to unresolved conflict
    • Regression: Going back to earlier stage under stress
    • Sublimation: Channeling sexual energy into productive work

    Connection to Other Topics

    → Connect to ERIKSON'S THEORY - Both propose stage development - Erikson expanded Freud's approach to full lifespan - Erikson focused on social relationships; Freud on sexual drives

    → Connect to DEFENCE MECHANISMS - Fixations cause ego to use defence mechanisms - Anal fixation individuals use rationalization and reaction formation

    → Connect to NORMAL vs ABNORMAL BEHAVIOR - Severe fixations → neurotic symptoms - Understanding stage helps explain client's current behavior patterns

    → ARISTOTLE CONNECTION - Aristotle: Development = achieving virtue at each life stage - Freud: Development = resolving conflict at each psychosexual stage - Both: Childhood experiences shape adult character

    TOPIC 2: ERIKSON'S PSYCHOSOCIAL DEVELOPMENT THEORY

    Simple Explanation

    Erik Erikson built on Freud's work but made 3 major changes: 1. Extended development to ENTIRE LIFESPAN (not just childhood) 2. Focused on SOCIAL relationships, not sexual drives 3. Each stage has a CRISIS (challenge) that must be resolved

    Each stage presents two opposite forces. Success at resolving them creates psychological strength; failure creates weakness.

    The 8 Stages:

    Stage Age Crisis Success Outcome Failure Outcome
    1 0-18 months Trust vs Mistrust Hope, security Fear, anxiety
    2 18 months-3 years Autonomy vs Shame/Doubt Will, independence Shame, self-doubt
    3 3-5 years Initiative vs Guilt Purpose, confidence Guilt, inhibition
    4 6 years-puberty Industry vs Inferiority Competence, skill Inferiority complex
    5 12-18 years Identity vs Role Confusion Fidelity, identity Confusion, uncertainty
    6 18-25 years Intimacy vs Isolation Love, relationships Loneliness, isolation
    7 25-65 years Generativity vs Stagnation Care, contribution Selfishness, stagnation
    8 65+ years Ego Integrity vs Despair Wisdom, acceptance Despair, regret

    Stage-by-Stage Explanation

    Stage 1: TRUST vs MISTRUST (Infancy) - Baby depends completely on caregivers - If caregivers respond to needs: Baby develops trust, feels safe - If caregivers neglect: Baby develops mistrust, feels unsafe - For PSW: Understanding client's basic trust issues; impact of early neglect

    Stage 4: INDUSTRY vs INFERIORITY (School Age) - Child learns skills through school and activities - Success → feels competent, worthy - Repeated failure → develops inferiority complex - For PSW: Why some children give up; importance of success experiences

    Stage 5: IDENTITY vs ROLE CONFUSION (Adolescence) - Most critical for Psychiatric Social Work! - Adolescent asks: "Who am I? What do I believe? Where do I fit?" - Success → clear sense of identity, direction - Failure → identity confusion, unclear values, role confusion - For PSW: Reason for adolescent mental health issues; identity crisis common

    Stage 6: INTIMACY vs ISOLATION (Young Adulthood) - Young adult seeks close relationships - Can only achieve intimacy after identity is formed (Stage 5) - Success → meaningful relationships, love - Failure → loneliness, isolation, fear of commitment

    Stage 7: GENERATIVITY vs STAGNATION (Middle Adulthood) - Person contributes to next generation (children, work, mentoring) - Success → feels life has meaning, leaves legacy - Failure → feels life is pointless, self-centered - For PSW: Understand midlife crisis; importance of meaningful work

    Most Asked Questions

    • Which stage involves "Who am I?" question? (Stage 5: Adolescence)
    • Trust vs Mistrust develops in which stage? (Stage 1: Infancy)
    • Intimacy vs Isolation is in which stage? (Stage 6: Young Adulthood)
    • How many stages in Erikson's theory? (8 stages)
    • What is generativity? (Contributing to next generation)

    Connection to Other Topics

    → Connect to FREUD'S THEORY - Erikson based on Freud but extended it - Erikson less focus on sexual drives, more on social needs - Both: Early experiences matter; stage-based development

    → Connect to ATTACHMENT THEORY - Trust vs Mistrust = Secure vs Insecure attachment - Early bonding affects later relationships

    → Connect to MENTAL HEALTH - Erikson's framework explains common mental health issues - Identity confusion → depression, anxiety in adolescents - Lack of generativity → midlife depression

    → ARISTOTLE CONNECTION - Aristotle: "We are what we repeatedly do" - Erikson: Repeated experiences at each stage shape personality - Both: Development = gradual formation of character

    TOPIC 3: MENTAL HEALTH & MENTAL HYGIENE CONCEPTS

    Simple Explanation

    MENTAL HEALTH (WHO Definition): "A state of mental well-being in which an individual realizes their abilities, can cope with normal life stresses, can work productively, and contribute to their community."

    KEY POINT: Mental health ≠ Just "no mental illness" - It's POSITIVE state, not just absence of disease - It's a SPECTRUM (not black/white) - It includes: emotional, psychological, social well-being

    MENTAL HYGIENE: Similar to PHYSICAL hygiene (brushing teeth, bathing), mental hygiene is proactive care to maintain mental health and prevent mental illness.

    The 3 Pillars of Mental Health

    1. EMOTIONAL WELL-BEING
    2. Understanding and managing emotions
    3. Resilience under stress

    4. Life satisfaction

    5. PSYCHOLOGICAL WELL-BEING

    6. Self-actualization and personal growth
    7. Meaning and purpose

    8. Autonomy and decision-making

    9. SOCIAL WELL-BEING

    10. Quality relationships
    11. Social support
    12. Community contribution
    13. Social inclusion

    Mental Hygiene Principles (Prevention)

    Physical Health Foundation: - Adequate sleep (7-9 hours) - Proper nutrition - Regular physical exercise - Limit alcohol, avoid drugs

    Psychological Practices: - Stress management techniques (meditation, breathing) - Mindfulness - Mental retraining (positive thinking) - Regular self-reflection

    Social Practices: - Maintain close relationships - Join community groups - Help others (volunteering) - Seek social support

    Professional Care: - Regular check-ups with mental health professionals - Early intervention if symptoms appear - Counseling/therapy when needed

    Characteristics of Mental Health

    ✓ Realistic perception of reality ✓ Ability to control impulses ✓ Effective coping with stress ✓ Good self-esteem ✓ Ability to love and form relationships ✓ Productive work and learning ✓ Sense of purpose and meaning

    Most Asked Questions

    • What is WHO definition of mental health? (State of well-being where individual realizes abilities)
    • Is mental health just absence of mental illness? (No, it's positive state)
    • Name one mental hygiene principle? (Sleep, nutrition, exercise, relationships, etc.)
    • What are 3 pillars of mental health? (Emotional, Psychological, Social)
    • Mental hygiene aims to what? (Promote mental health and prevent illness)

    Connection to Other Topics

    → Connect to PERSONALITY THEORIES - Personality affects mental health outcomes - Erikson's successful development → better mental health

    → Connect to NORMAL vs ABNORMAL BEHAVIOR - Mental health exists on CONTINUUM - Most people experience mental health challenges sometimes

    → Connect to SOCIAL SUPPORT - Social relationships crucial for mental health - Isolation → mental health problems

    → Connect to MENTAL HEALTH PROGRAMS (DMHP, MHCA) - Implementation of mental health promotion - Government schemes supporting mental hygiene

    → ARISTOTLE CONNECTION - Aristotle's "Eudaimonia" (Human Flourishing) = Modern Mental Health concept - Both emphasize realizing human potential and living well - Both require developing virtues and good character

    TOPIC 4: SOCIAL WORK METHODS: CASEWORK, GROUP WORK, COMMUNITY ORGANIZATION

    Simple Explanation

    Social workers use different methods (ways) to help. Think of it like a doctor using different treatments for different patients.

    PRIMARY METHODS (Direct work with people): 1. Case Work (1-on-1) 2. Group Work (Groups) 3. Community Organization (Community level)

    SECONDARY METHODS (Supporting/Administrative): 1. Social Welfare Administration 2. Social Work Research 3. Social Action

    METHOD 1: CASE WORK (Individual/Family Work)

    What is it? One-on-one helping relationship between social worker and client (or family).

    Process (4 Steps): 1. Assessment - Understand client's problems, strengths, resources 2. Planning - Set goals with client 3. Intervention - Take action to solve problems 4. Evaluation - Check if goals achieved

    Tools Used: - Interviews and listening - Building trust - Problem-solving - Counseling - Resource linking

    Types of Case Work: - Individual Casework: One person - Family Casework: Whole family unit (domestic violence, parenting issues, family conflict) - Group Casework: Multiple individuals with similar problems in group setting

    Example: A psychiatric social worker helps a person with depression: assess triggers, plan treatment, refer to psychiatrist, follow-up to ensure improvement.

    METHOD 2: GROUP WORK

    What is it? Using group dynamics (power of group) to help individuals improve, learn, and support each other.

    Principle: "Strength in numbers" - People help each other in group setting.

    Types of Groups:

    1. Therapeutic Groups
    2. Purpose: Healing and growth
    3. Example: Group therapy for trauma survivors

    4. Facilitator: Social worker guides but doesn't direct

    5. Support Groups

    6. Purpose: Mutual support for common issues
    7. Example: Cancer survivor support group, grief support group

    8. Facilitator: Often peer-led, social worker coordinates

    9. Skill-Building Groups

    10. Purpose: Learn specific skills
    11. Example: Anger management group, parenting skills group

    12. Facilitator: Social worker teaches specific skills

    13. Educational Groups

    14. Purpose: Share information
    15. Example: Mental health awareness group
    16. Facilitator: Social worker provides education

    Benefits of Group Work: - Reduced isolation (realize "I'm not alone") - Peer learning - Social skills practice - Cost-effective - Empowerment through mutual help

    METHOD 3: COMMUNITY ORGANIZATION

    What is it? Organizing communities to identify problems, plan solutions, and take collective action.

    Goal: Sustainable social change through community participation.

    Process: 1. Community Assessment - Understand community needs 2. Awareness Building - Educate community about issues 3. Mobilization - Encourage participation 4. Action - Take collective steps 5. Sustainability - Maintain changes

    Tools: - Community meetings - Focus group discussions - Participatory Rural Appraisal (PRA) - Advocacy campaigns - Capacity building - Network development

    Example: PSW organizing community meeting to discuss mental health stigma, planning awareness campaign, mobilizing community leaders, conducting workshops.

    vs CASE WORK Comparison: - Case work: Individual focus, immediate problems - Community org: Group focus, structural/systemic change

    SECONDARY METHODS (Brief Overview)

    Social Welfare Administration - Management of programs and services - Planning, budgeting, staff coordination - Ensuring effective service delivery

    Social Work Research - Systematic investigation of social problems - Evaluating intervention effectiveness - Contributing to knowledge

    Social Action - Advocacy for policy change - Challenging unjust systems - Social justice focus

    Most Asked Questions

    • What is Case Work? (1-on-1 helping relationship)
    • Group Work is based on what principle? (Group dynamics, mutual help)
    • Community Organization aims to what? (Sustainable community change)
    • Which method is PRIMARY method of social work? (Case work, group work, community org)
    • Difference between case work and community org? (Individual vs community; immediate vs systemic)

    Connection to Other Topics

    → Connect to SOCIAL WORK PRINCIPLES - All methods guided by principles of acceptance, self-determination, confidentiality - Methods differ but principles same

    → Connect to THERAPEUTIC CONCEPTS - Transference/counter-transference occur in all methods - Understanding therapeutic relationship crucial

    → Connect to ASSESSMENT AND DIAGNOSIS - Each method requires different assessment - Case work: individual assessment; Community org: community assessment

    → ARISTOTLE CONNECTION - Aristotle emphasized practical wisdom (phronesis) in acting - Social worker uses practical wisdom to choose right method for situation - Virtue ethics: Social worker develops virtues in different methods

    TOPIC 5: DEFENCE MECHANISMS & PSYCHOLOGICAL COPING

    Simple Explanation

    When facing stressful or threatening situations, our mind uses defence mechanisms - automatic, unconscious tricks to protect our ego (self-image) from anxiety.

    Key Point: Defence mechanisms are NOT conscious; we don't realize we're using them.

    The 3 Levels of Defence Mechanisms

    LEVEL 1: IMMATURE DEFENCES (Used by children and adults under stress)

    1. PROJECTION
    2. What: Attributing own unacceptable feelings to others
    3. Example: "I'm not angry at you, YOU are angry at me"

    4. Real Situation: Aggressive person accuses others of being aggressive

    5. DISPLACEMENT

    6. What: Expressing feelings toward safe target instead of real target
    7. Example: Boss criticizes you at work; you go home and yell at spouse

    8. Real Situation: Moving emotional reaction from real source to substitute

    9. ACTING OUT

    10. What: Expressing unconscious impulses through actions instead of words
    11. Example: Angry person punches wall instead of talking about anger

    12. Real Situation: Physical expression of emotions instead of verbal

    13. REGRESSION

    14. What: Going back to earlier, childish behaviors
    15. Example: Adult sucking thumb when stressed; reverting to childlike speech

    16. Real Situation: Under extreme stress, acting younger than actual age

    17. IDENTIFICATION

    18. What: Adopting characteristics of someone else to reduce anxiety
    19. Example: Abused child copying abuser's behavior
    20. Real Situation: Incorporating another's traits into own personality

    LEVEL 2: NEUROTIC DEFENCES (Used by most people sometimes)

    1. REPRESSION
    2. What: Pushing threatening memories into unconscious
    3. Example: Childhood abuse victim "forgetting" the abuse
    4. Real Situation: Can't remember event even though it happened

    5. Note: Different from SUPPRESSION (conscious forgetting)

    6. RATIONALIZATION

    7. What: Creating logical explanations for illogical feelings/behaviors
    8. Example: "I'm late because traffic was bad" (when really you overslept)

    9. Real Situation: Justifying behavior with false reasoning

    10. INTELLECTUALIZATION

    11. What: Discussing emotional topics in detached, intellectual way
    12. Example: Person discussing death of loved one without any emotion

    13. Real Situation: Avoiding emotional pain through intellectual analysis

    14. REACTION FORMATION

    15. What: Expressing opposite of what you truly feel
    16. Example: Overly nice to person you actually hate
    17. Real Situation: Masking true emotion with opposite emotion

    LEVEL 3: MATURE DEFENCES (Adaptive, healthy; used by psychologically healthy people)

    1. SUBLIMATION
    2. What: Channeling negative emotions/energy into productive activities
    3. Example: Processing anger through sports, art, music, or work
    4. Real Situation: Converting emotional pain into creative output

    5. Most Healthy Defence: Acknowledged and studied extensively

    6. HUMOUR (Appropriate, not hurtful)

    7. What: Using humor to cope with stress without hurting others
    8. Example: Making light joke about your own mistake
    9. Real Situation: Laugh at situation to reduce tension

    10. Not: Making fun of others or cruel jokes

    11. SUPPRESSION

    12. What: Consciously postponing dealing with impulses/conflicts
    13. Example: "I'm upset about something but I'll talk about it after the meeting"

    14. Real Situation: Conscious decision to delay processing emotion

    15. ALTRUISM

    16. What: Helping others to work through own conflicts
    17. Example: Working with homeless population after own difficult period

    18. Real Situation: Turning own suffering into service

    19. ANTICIPATION

    20. What: Realistic planning for future challenges
    21. Example: Worrying about exam so you study hard
    22. Real Situation: Anxiety motivates preparation

    Most Asked Questions

    • Attributing own feelings to others is? (Projection)
    • Pushing memories into unconscious is? (Repression)
    • Converting anger into sports is? (Sublimation)
    • Which is mature defence? (Sublimation, humor, altruism)
    • Defence mechanisms are? (Unconscious processes protecting ego)

    Connection to Other Topics

    → Connect to FREUD'S PSYCHOSEXUAL DEVELOPMENT - Each stage has typical defences - Fixation at stage → rigid use of certain defences - Anal fixation → reaction formation, obsessive defences

    → Connect to THERAPEUTIC RELATIONSHIP - PSW identifies client's defences - Does NOT directly attack defences - Works with defences to understand underlying conflict - Goal: Help move from immature to mature defences

    → Connect to ABNORMAL BEHAVIOR - Excessive/rigid use of defences → neurotic symptoms - Understanding defences helps understand symptoms

    → ARISTOTLE CONNECTION - Virtue ethics: Mature defences reflect wise judgment - Aristotle's practical wisdom = using appropriate defence for situation - Mature person knows when to use humor vs when to face reality

    TOPIC 6: NORMAL vs ABNORMAL BEHAVIOR

    Simple Explanation

    Question: When is behavior "abnormal"?

    Answer: When it's MALADAPTIVE, causes DISTRESS, and violates SOCIAL NORMS.

    Key Point: No single criterion defines abnormality. Use MULTIPLE PERSPECTIVES.

    4 Criteria for Defining Abnormality

    Criterion Description Limitation
    Statistical Rarity Behavior is statistically unusual/rare Genius-level intelligence is rare but normal
    Personal Distress Behavior causes suffering to person Some disturbed people don't feel distressed
    Violation of Social Norms Behavior breaks social rules Social norms vary by culture and time
    Maladaptation/Dysfunction Behavior prevents functioning What counts as dysfunction varies

    Three Models of Abnormality

    MODEL 1: MEDICAL MODEL - View: Abnormality = mental illness = brain disease - Cause: Biological (genetic, neurochemical) - Treatment: Medical (drugs, hospitalization) - Problem: Ignores psychological and social factors

    MODEL 2: PSYCHOLOGICAL MODEL - View: Abnormality = learned maladaptive behavior or thought patterns - Cause: Past experiences, trauma, learned associations - Treatment: Therapy, counseling, behavior modification - Problem: Ignores biological and social factors

    MODEL 3: SOCIAL MODEL - View: Abnormality = failure to adapt to society's expectations - Cause: Social factors (poverty, discrimination, stress) - Treatment: Social change, environmental modifications - Problem: May ignore individual pathology

    BIOPSYCHOSOCIAL MODEL (Recommended approach)

    Integrated Model considering all three factors:

    Biological Factors
(genetics, brain chemistry, neurotransmitters)
          ↓
     ↙    ↓    ↘
Psychological ← ABNORMALITY → Social Factors
Factors               (environment, culture,
(trauma, beliefs,      socioeconomic, relationships)
learning)

    Example - Depression: - Biological: Low serotonin - Psychological: Negative thought patterns, past trauma - Social: Unemployment, relationship problems, isolation

    Treatment: Combination of medication (biological), therapy (psychological), social support (social).

    Examples of Normal Behavior That Might Seem Abnormal

    • Grief after loss: Sad, withdrawn, crying = NORMAL (not depression unless severe)
    • Anxiety before exam: Nervous, worried = NORMAL
    • Shyness: Introversion, social anxiety = NORMAL (unless severe social phobia)
    • Belief Systems: Religious practices, cultural rituals = NORMAL if culturally appropriate

    Examples of Abnormal Behavior

    • Hallucinations: Hearing voices others can't hear + causes distress = ABNORMAL
    • Delusions: Believing things against all evidence = ABNORMAL
    • Compulsions: Uncontrollable repetitive behaviors = ABNORMAL
    • Parasuicidal: Self-harm to cope = ABNORMAL

    Assessment Tools for Abnormality

    1. Clinical Interview: Professional conversation
    2. Psychological Tests: IQ tests, personality tests
    3. Medical Tests: Blood tests, brain imaging
    4. Social Assessment: Family, work, social functioning
    5. DSM-5 / ICD-11: Diagnostic manuals

    Most Asked Questions

    • Abnormality defined by what criteria? (Multiple: distress, dysfunction, deviance, rarity)
    • Statistical rarity alone defines abnormality? (No)
    • Biopsychosocial model includes what? (Biological, Psychological, Social)
    • Grief after loss is abnormal? (No, it's normal unless severe)
    • Three models of abnormality are? (Medical, Psychological, Social)

    Connection to Other Topics

    → Connect to MENTAL HEALTH CONCEPTS - Normal-abnormal on CONTINUUM - Mental health is more than just "no abnormality"

    → Connect to PERSONALITY THEORIES - Understanding development explains variations in behavior - Fixations (Freud) or unresolved crises (Erikson) → abnormal patterns

    → Connect to DEFENCE MECHANISMS - Using immature/rigid defences → appears abnormal - Understanding defences contextualizes behavior

    → Connect to PSYCHIATRIC DIAGNOSIS - Abnormality assessment → DSM-5 diagnosis - PSW must understand what's normal in context

    → ARISTOTLE CONNECTION - Aristotle: Virtue = mean between extremes - Abnormality = Extreme behavior (too much or too little) - Virtue (normal) = Balanced response - Example: Courage = between cowardice and recklessness

    TOPIC 7: SOCIAL WORK PRINCIPLES & PROFESSIONAL VALUES

    Simple Explanation

    Principles are the FUNDAMENTAL GUIDELINES that govern ALL social work practice, regardless of method or setting.

    Think of them like the "rules" of ethical practice that social workers must follow.

    7 Core Principles of Social Work

    1. PRINCIPLE OF INDIVIDUALIZATION

    What: Treat each client as unique person; no two cases are identical.

    How to Apply: - Assess unique factors: age, culture, beliefs, abilities, challenges - Don't use "one-size-fits-all" solutions - Tailor intervention to specific client - Recognize individual differences despite similar presenting problems

    Example: Two clients with depression may need different approaches - Client A: Needs medication + cognitive therapy - Client B: Needs counseling + social support + job training

    2. PRINCIPLE OF PURPOSEFUL EXPRESSION

    What: Create conditions where client can freely express thoughts, feelings, and concerns.

    How to Apply: - Active listening without interruption - Create safe, non-judgmental environment - Ask open-ended questions - Respect client's pace of sharing - Use body language showing interest

    Example: Let client tell their story fully before offering advice; don't rush or interrupt.

    3. PRINCIPLE OF CONTROLLED EMOTIONAL INVOLVEMENT

    What: Show empathy and caring WITHOUT losing objectivity and professional boundaries.

    How to Apply: - Empathy ≠ Sympathy (understand vs feel sorry for) - Maintain professional distance - Be warm but not personal friends - Manage counter-transference - Know when to refer to another worker if too emotionally involved

    Tricky Balance: Care about client BUT don't get too involved that judgment becomes clouded.

    Example: "I understand this is painful for you" (empathy) vs "That's terrible, I feel so sad for you" (too much sympathy).

    4. PRINCIPLE OF ACCEPTANCE

    What: Respect client's worth and dignity REGARDLESS of personal views about their behavior/beliefs.

    How to Apply: - Separate person from behavior - Accept client even if you disagree with actions - Don't judge or moralize - Create safe space where client feels valued - Accept client's pace of change

    NOT Condoning: You accept the person but may not accept all behaviors.

    Example: Client is ex-convict or sex worker. Accept them as worthy person even if you don't approve of past actions.

    5. PRINCIPLE OF NON-JUDGMENTAL ATTITUDE

    What: Set aside personal biases, prejudices, and moral judgments in assessment and treatment.

    How to Apply: - Be aware of own biases - Actively work to suspend judgment - Assess objectively based on facts - Respect diverse values and beliefs - Recognize cultural differences - Don't impose own values

    Hardest Principle: Requires ongoing self-awareness and reflection.

    Example: Client believes in arranged marriage. Even if you prefer love marriage, don't judge or criticize their choice.

    6. PRINCIPLE OF CONFIDENTIALITY

    What: Protect client's privacy. Information shared in professional relationship is confidential.

    How to Apply: - Keep client information private - Don't gossip or share unnecessarily - Write records securely - Seek permission before sharing - Know exceptions: Risk of harm, abuse, court orders, breaking law

    Important Exception: If client is danger to self/others or there's abuse, MUST breach confidentiality.

    Example: Client tells you about suicidal plans. Must inform psychiatrist/family despite confidentiality principle.

    7. PRINCIPLE OF SELF-DETERMINATION

    What: Respect client's right to make own decisions about their life.

    How to Apply: - Provide information about options - Support client's choices (even if you disagree) - Don't impose solutions - Client drives pace and goals of intervention - Recognize client's autonomy

    Limit: When choice causes harm (endangering self/others), may need to limit self-determination.

    Example: Client decides to leave husband. Even if you think she should stay, support her decision if it's her choice.

    Core Professional Values

    1. HUMAN DIGNITY - Every person has worth - Respect for all human beings

    2. SOCIAL JUSTICE - Fair treatment regardless of status - Advocacy for oppressed/vulnerable - Challenge systemic inequalities

    3. RESPECT FOR DIVERSITY - Cultural competence - Respecting different beliefs, backgrounds - Non-discrimination

    4. EMPOWERMENT - Enable clients to take control of life - Develop strengths and capacities - Reduce dependency

    5. COMMITMENT TO SERVICE - Dedication to helping - Going beyond minimum requirements - Putting client needs first

    Most Asked Questions

    • What is principle of acceptance? (Respecting client's worth despite disagreeing with actions)
    • Confidentiality can be breached when? (Risk of harm, abuse, court order)
    • Difference between empathy and sympathy? (Empathy: understanding; Sympathy: feeling sorry)
    • Non-judgmental attitude means? (Setting aside personal biases and moral judgments)
    • Self-determination principle means? (Client's right to make own decisions)

    TOPIC 8: THERAPEUTIC CONCEPTS - TRANSFERENCE & COUNTER-TRANSFERENCE

    Simple Explanation

    TRANSFERENCE: Client's emotional reaction to therapist BASED ON PAST RELATIONSHIPS, not based on therapist's actual behavior.

    COUNTER-TRANSFERENCE: Therapist's emotional reaction to client.

    Both are NORMAL, EXPECTED, and therapeutically USEFUL if recognized and managed.

    TRANSFERENCE in Detail

    What is Transference?

    Client projects feelings, attitudes, expectations from past relationships (especially parents/authority figures) ONTO the therapist/social worker.

    Key Point: It's about CLIENT'S PAST, not about therapist's actual behavior.

    Common Transference Patterns:

    1. Paternal/Maternal Transference
    2. Client treats worker like parent
    3. Seeks approval/disapproval like child

    4. Example: Adult client seeking validation from worker like seeking from parent

    5. Romantic Transference

    6. Client develops attraction or romantic feelings
    7. Based on client's needs, not real relationship

    8. Example: Client idealizing worker, wanting closer relationship

    9. Negative Transference

    10. Client becomes angry, resistant, suspicious
    11. Transfers past negative experiences onto worker

    12. Example: Client antagonistic if worker reminds them of authority figure who hurt them

    13. Authoritative Transference

    14. Client sees worker as expert/authority
    15. Complies or resists against authority
    16. Example: Client follows all advice unthinkingly or rebels against all suggestions

    COUNTER-TRANSFERENCE in Detail

    What is Counter-transference?

    Therapist's emotional and behavioral responses to client, including: - Feelings (attraction, anger, boredom) - Reactions (wanting to rescue, becoming punitive) - Biases triggered by client

    Why Counter-transference Happens? - Therapist is human with own issues - Client reminds therapist of someone from their past - Client's behavior triggers therapist's vulnerabilities

    Examples of Counter-transference: - Therapist attracted to client - Therapist overprotective of vulnerable client - Therapist impatient with demanding client - Therapist identifying too much with client's struggles - Therapist avoiding certain clients

    How to Manage Transference & Counter-transference

    Steps to Manage TRANSFERENCE:

    1. RECOGNIZE IT
    2. Notice unusual emotional reactions from client
    3. Client's behavior inconsistent with situation

    4. Patterns emerging

    5. UNDERSTAND IT

    6. Where is this coming from?
    7. What past experience is being repeated?

    8. What need is client expressing?

    9. USE IT THERAPEUTICALLY

    10. Gently point out pattern: "I notice you seem angry with me like..."
    11. Help client understand connection to past

    12. Insight → Change in patterns

    13. DON'T ACT ON IT

    14. Don't reciprocate romantic transference
    15. Don't become the idealized parent
    16. Maintain professional boundaries
    17. Don't collude with resistance

    Steps to Manage COUNTER-TRANSFERENCE:

    1. RECOGNIZE YOUR FEELINGS
    2. Be aware of emotional reactions
    3. Identify what specifically triggers you

    4. Don't act immediately

    5. REFLECT ON SOURCE

    6. Why does this client trigger me?
    7. What do they remind me of?

    8. What's MY issue here?

    9. MANAGE IT PROFESSIONALLY

    10. Don't let feelings guide your treatment
    11. Maintain objectivity
    12. Seek supervision if needed

    13. Sometimes refer to another worker if too involved

    14. USE IT FOR UNDERSTANDING

    15. Counter-transference can provide insight into client
    16. If you feel rejected, maybe client rejects others too
    17. Clues about client's impact on relationships

    Examples Showing Both

    Scenario: Female client with absent father meets male social worker.

    Client's Transference (to worker): - Seeks approval/attention - Tests if he'll abandon her like father - Initially idealized; later disappointed - Angry or withdrawn if he doesn't respond emotionally

    Worker's Counter-transference (to client): - Feels protective/parental - Wants to "fix" her father issues - Gets frustrated if she doesn't improve - Notices he looks forward to her sessions

    Healthy Management: - Worker recognizes patterns - Helps client see: "You seem to want approval from me like you wanted from father" - Worker maintains professional boundaries - Supports client's healing of father relationship

    Most Asked Questions

    • Transference is? (Client's projection of past onto therapist)
    • Counter-transference is? (Therapist's emotional reaction to client)
    • Transference is problem or useful? (Both - problem if not managed; useful if recognized)
    • Should therapist act on counter-transference? (No, must recognize and manage it)
    • How to identify transference? (Notice unusual emotional reactions, patterns)
