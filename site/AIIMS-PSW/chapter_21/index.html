    Chapter 21: Attitudes, Prejudice & Discrimination

    Learning Objectives

    Upon completing this chapter, you will be able to: * Define what an attitude is and describe its three components using the ABC model. * Explain the difference between stereotypes, prejudice, and discrimination. * Identify the social, emotional, and cognitive roots of prejudice. * Describe effective strategies for reducing prejudice, such as the contact hypothesis. * Understand how these concepts relate to stigma and social justice issues in social work.

    Exam Relevance

    • Mark Weight: High. These are core concepts in social psychology. Expect 2-3 questions focusing on the ABC model of attitudes and the distinction between prejudice and discrimination.
    • Topic Category: Subject Knowledge - Social Psychology.
    • Importance: Critical. Understanding how attitudes, prejudice, and discrimination operate is fundamental for any social worker committed to social justice, anti-oppressive practice, and combating stigma.

    Detailed Theory / Concepts

    What is an Attitude?

    In social psychology, an attitude is a learned evaluation of a person, object, or issue. It influences our thoughts and actions. Attitudes can be positive, negative, or neutral.

    The ABC Model of Attitudes

    Attitudes are generally understood to have three components:

    1. A - Affective Component: This refers to your feelings or emotional reactions toward the attitude object.

      • Example: Fear of snakes. The fear is the affective component.

    2. B - Behavioral Component: This is the way you act or behave in relation to the attitude object.

      • Example: Running away when you see a snake. The action of running is the behavioral component.

    3. C - Cognitive Component: This consists of your thoughts and beliefs about the attitude object.

      • Example: The belief that "all snakes are dangerous." The belief is the cognitive component.

    Prejudice, Stereotypes, and Discrimination

    These three concepts are related to negative attitudes but are distinct.

    Concept Component Definition Example
    Stereotype Cognitive (Belief) A generalized belief about a group of people. It's a mental shortcut that assigns certain traits to all members of a group, ignoring individual differences. "All librarians are quiet and shy."
    Prejudice Affective (Feeling) A preconceived negative feeling or attitude towards a person based solely on their membership in a particular group. Feeling dislike or suspicion towards someone simply because of their nationality.
    Discrimination Behavioral (Action) Unjustifiable negative behavior or action towards a member of a group. It is prejudice put into action. Denying someone a job or housing because of their race, religion, or gender.

    The Relationship: Stereotypes (beliefs) can fuel Prejudice (feelings), which can lead to Discrimination (actions).

    What are the Sources of Prejudice?

    Prejudice doesn't just appear out of nowhere. It has deep roots.

    1. Social Sources:

      • Social Inequality: Prejudice can be used by dominant groups to justify existing social hierarchies and inequalities. For example, believing a group is "naturally inferior" can be used to justify paying them lower wages.
      • In-group and Out-group: We have a natural tendency to divide the world into "us" (the in-group) and "them" (the out-group). We often exhibit in-group bias, which is the tendency to favor our own group.

    2. Emotional Sources:

      • Scapegoat Theory: This theory suggests that when things go wrong, people may look for someone to blame. Frustration and aggression are often displaced onto a vulnerable group (the scapegoat) that is not the real source of the problem. For example, blaming immigrants for economic downturns.

    3. Cognitive Sources:

      • Categorization: Our brains naturally categorize information to simplify the world. When we categorize people, we often overestimate the similarities within groups and the differences between groups, which is the foundation of stereotyping.
      • Just-World Phenomenon: This is the tendency for people to believe that the world is just and that people, therefore, get what they deserve and deserve what they get. This can lead to blaming the victim ("She was poor because she was lazy").

    How Can Prejudice Be Reduced?

    • The Contact Hypothesis: Proposed by Gordon Allport, this is one of the most influential theories. It suggests that prejudice can be reduced by direct contact between groups, but only under specific conditions:
      1. Equal Status: Both groups must have equal status in the situation.
      2. Common Goals: Both groups must work together towards a shared goal.
      3. Intergroup Cooperation: The situation must require cooperation, not competition.
      4. Institutional Support: The contact must be supported by authorities, laws, or customs.
    • Education: Educating people about different cultures and the realities of prejudice can help reduce stereotypes.
    • Empathy: Encouraging people to take the perspective of an out-group member can increase empathy and reduce prejudice.

    AIIMS CRE-Oriented Tips & Tricks

    • ABC Model: Remember Affect (Feeling), Behavior (Action), Cognition (Belief).
    • Prejudice vs. Discrimination: This is the most critical distinction. Prejudice is a feeling/attitude. Discrimination is an action/behavior.
    • Scapegoat Theory: This is a frequently tested concept. It's about blaming a powerless out-group for the in-group's problems.
    • Contact Hypothesis Conditions: Remember that simple contact is not enough to reduce prejudice. The conditions (equal status, common goals, etc.) are crucial.

    Practice MCQs

    1. The emotional or feeling component of an attitude is the: a) Cognitive component b) Behavioral component c) Affective component d) Social component

    2. A generalized belief about a group of people, such as "all politicians are corrupt," is an example of a(n): a) Prejudice b) Stereotype c) Discrimination d) Attitude

    3. Refusing to rent an apartment to someone because of their race is an example of: a) Prejudice b) Stereotyping c) Discrimination d) Scapegoating

    4. The theory that explains prejudice as a result of blaming a vulnerable group for one's own frustrations is known as: a) Contact Hypothesis b) Just-World Phenomenon c) Scapegoat Theory d) In-group Bias

    5. Which of the following is NOT a necessary condition for the Contact Hypothesis to be effective in reducing prejudice? a) Equal status between groups b) Competition between groups c) Common goals d) Institutional support

    6. The belief that "people get what they deserve" and that victims of misfortune must have done something to deserve it is called the: a) Scapegoat Theory b) Just-World Phenomenon c) In-group Bias d) ABC Model

    7. Prejudice is to discrimination as __ is to ____. a) feeling; action b) action; feeling c) belief; feeling d) action; belief

    8. A person's belief that "exercise is good for health" represents which component of their attitude towards exercise? a) Affective b) Behavioral c) Cognitive d) Personal

    9. The tendency to favor one's own group over other groups is called: a) Stereotyping b) Scapegoating c) In-group bias d) Discrimination

    10. According to the ABC model, a person's intention to vote for a candidate is an example of the: a) Affective component b) Behavioral component c) Cognitive component d) Political component

    Answer Key with Explanations

    1. c) Affective component: The affective component is all about feelings and emotions.
    2. b) Stereotype: A stereotype is a generalized belief (a cognitive shortcut) about a group.
    3. c) Discrimination: This is a negative action or behavior based on group membership.
    4. c) Scapegoat Theory: This theory posits that frustration leads to aggression, which is often directed at a convenient and powerless target (a scapegoat).
    5. b) Competition between groups: The Contact Hypothesis requires cooperation, not competition, to be effective.
    6. b) Just-World Phenomenon: This cognitive bias can lead to victim-blaming and helps people maintain a sense of order and justice in the world.
    7. a) feeling; action: Prejudice is a negative feeling or attitude, while discrimination is the negative action or behavior that results from it.
    8. c) Cognitive: This is a thought or belief about exercise, which is the cognitive component of the attitude.
    9. c) In-group bias: This is the well-documented phenomenon of favoring members of one's own group.
    10. b) Behavioral component: An intention to act, like voting, is part of the behavioral component, which also includes the action itself.

    Chapter Summary (Key Takeaways)

    • Attitudes are evaluations of things and are composed of Affective (feeling), Behavioral (action), and Cognitive (belief) components.
    • Stereotypes are beliefs, Prejudice is a feeling, and Discrimination is an action. This is a critical distinction.
    • Prejudice arises from social inequalities, emotional needs (like scapegoating), and cognitive shortcuts (like categorization).
    • Prejudice can be reduced through intergroup contact, but only under specific conditions of equality, cooperation, and shared goals.
    • As a social worker, understanding these dynamics is key to challenging stigma and advocating for social justice.
