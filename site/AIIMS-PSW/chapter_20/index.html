    Chapter 20: Personality Theories & Defence Mechanisms

    Learning Objectives

    Upon completing this chapter, you will be able to: * Define personality and describe its key characteristics. * Explain the core ideas of Freud's psychoanalytic theory, including the Id, Ego, and Superego. * Define and identify common psychological defence mechanisms with examples. * Briefly describe the main ideas of Humanistic and Trait theories of personality. * Recognize the "Big Five" personality traits (OCEAN).

    Exam Relevance

    • Mark Weight: High. Personality theories are a major topic in psychology. Expect 2-3 questions, with a very high probability of them being on Freud's structural model (Id, Ego, Superego) and defence mechanisms.
    • Topic Category: Subject Knowledge - Psychology & Mental Health.
    • Importance: Critical. These theories provide the fundamental frameworks for understanding why people think, feel, and behave the way they do, which is central to any therapeutic or assessment role.

    Detailed Theory / Concepts

    What is Personality?

    Personality refers to an individual's unique and relatively stable patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving. It's the combination of characteristics or qualities that form an individual's distinctive character.

    1. Psychoanalytic Theory (Sigmund Freud)

    Freud's theory was the first comprehensive theory of personality, and it emphasized the influence of the unconscious mind.

    Levels of Consciousness

    Freud compared the mind to an iceberg: * The Conscious: The small part above the water. It's what we are aware of at any given moment. * The Preconscious: The area just below the surface. It contains thoughts and memories that are not currently in consciousness but can be easily brought to mind. * The Unconscious: The vast, deep part of the iceberg. It is a reservoir of feelings, thoughts, urges, and memories that are outside of our conscious awareness. Freud believed the unconscious is the primary driver of our behavior.

    The Structure of Personality

    Freud proposed that personality is composed of three interacting systems:

    Component Operating Principle Description
    Id Pleasure Principle The primitive, instinctual part of the mind that contains sexual and aggressive drives. It is entirely unconscious and seeks immediate gratification of all desires and urges.
    Ego Reality Principle The "executive" of the personality. It develops to mediate between the unrealistic demands of the Id, the moralistic rules of the Superego, and the constraints of the real world.
    Superego Morality Principle The moral compass of the personality. It represents our internalized ideals and standards that we acquire from our parents and society. It is our conscience, striving for perfection.

    Intrapsychic Conflict: Anxiety is the result of the constant conflict between the Id's impulses and the Superego's prohibitions. The Ego's job is to manage this conflict.

    Defence Mechanisms

    When the Ego cannot resolve the conflict between the Id and Superego, it experiences anxiety. To protect itself, the Ego uses unconscious psychological strategies called defence mechanisms.

    Defence Mechanism Description Example
    Repression Pushing anxiety-provoking thoughts and feelings out of consciousness. (The most basic defence). A person who survived a traumatic car crash has no memory of the event.
    Denial Refusing to acknowledge an anxiety-provoking reality. A person diagnosed with a terminal illness insists the doctors are wrong.
    Projection Attributing one's own unacceptable impulses or qualities to others. A person who is cheating on their spouse constantly accuses their spouse of being unfaithful.
    Rationalization Creating logical-sounding excuses for unacceptable behavior. A student says, "I failed the test because the questions were unfair," instead of admitting they didn't study.
    Displacement Shifting impulses from an unacceptable target to a more acceptable or less threatening one. After being criticized by your boss, you go home and yell at your family member.
    Sublimation Channeling unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable and constructive activities. (Considered a mature defence). A person with strong aggressive urges becomes a competitive boxer.
    Reaction Formation Behaving in a way that is the exact opposite of one's true, unconscious feelings. A person who harbors secret homosexual feelings becomes a vocal homophobe.

    2. Humanistic Theory (Maslow & Rogers)

    This perspective emphasizes human potential, free will, and the drive for personal growth. * Abraham Maslow: Proposed the Hierarchy of Needs, suggesting that we are motivated to fulfill basic needs before moving on to higher needs, with self-actualization (achieving one's full potential) at the top. * Carl Rogers: Believed that for a person to "grow," they need an environment that provides genuineness, acceptance (which he called unconditional positive regard), and empathy.

    3. Trait Theory: The "Big Five"

    Trait theory focuses on identifying and measuring stable personality characteristics (traits). The most widely accepted model today is the "Big Five" or OCEAN model.

    • O - Openness to Experience: (Imaginative, curious vs. practical, conventional)
    • C - Conscientiousness: (Organized, disciplined vs. impulsive, careless)
    • E - Extraversion: (Sociable, fun-loving vs. sober, reserved)
    • A - Agreeableness: (Soft-hearted, trusting vs. ruthless, suspicious)
    • N - Neuroticism: (Anxious, insecure vs. calm, secure) (Also called Emotional Stability)

    AIIMS CRE-Oriented Tips & Tricks

    • Freud is High-Yield: The Id, Ego, Superego, and defence mechanisms are the most frequently tested concepts from this chapter.
    • Ego = Reality: Remember the Ego operates on the Reality Principle, mediating between the Id and Superego.
    • Defence Mechanisms are Unconscious: A key feature is that the person is not aware they are using them.
    • Sublimation is "Good": Sublimation is considered the most mature and healthy defence mechanism.
    • OCEAN: Use the acronym OCEAN to remember the Big Five traits.

    Practice MCQs

    1. According to Freud, which part of the personality operates on the "pleasure principle" and seeks immediate gratification? a) Ego b) Superego c) Id d) Preconscious

    2. A student who fails an exam and blames the teacher's teaching style instead of their own lack of preparation is using which defence mechanism? a) Repression b) Projection c) Rationalization d) Denial

    3. The "executive" of the personality, which mediates between the other two structures, is the: a) Id b) Ego c) Superego d) Unconscious

    4. A person with strong aggressive urges who becomes a successful surgeon is demonstrating: a) Reaction Formation b) Displacement c) Sublimation d) Projection

    5. Which of the following is NOT one of the "Big Five" personality traits? a) Openness b) Conscientiousness c) Agreeableness d) Intelligence

    6. Pushing distressing memories into the unconscious is known as: a) Denial b) Repression c) Projection d) Displacement

    7. According to Freud, the part of the personality that represents our conscience and moral standards is the: a) Id b) Ego c) Superego d) Self

    8. A person who is angry at their boss but goes home and yells at their dog is using: a) Rationalization b) Displacement c) Sublimation d) Reaction Formation

    9. The humanistic perspective on personality, championed by Maslow and Rogers, emphasizes: a) Unconscious conflicts b) The role of learning and conditioning c) The measurement of stable traits d) Free will and the drive for self-actualization

    10. Refusing to believe that one has a serious illness, despite clear medical evidence, is an example of: a) Denial b) Repression c) Projection d) Rationalization

    Answer Key with Explanations

    1. c) Id: The Id is the primitive, instinctual part of the personality that seeks immediate pleasure.
    2. c) Rationalization: The student is creating a plausible but false excuse to justify their failure and protect their ego.
    3. b) Ego: The Ego operates on the reality principle, balancing the demands of the Id, Superego, and the external world.
    4. c) Sublimation: This is the classic example of sublimation, where unacceptable impulses (aggression) are channeled into a socially valued and constructive profession.
    5. d) Intelligence: The Big Five traits are Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism (OCEAN). Intelligence is a separate construct.
    6. b) Repression: Repression is the fundamental defence mechanism of unconsciously pushing away painful thoughts or memories.
    7. c) Superego: The Superego is the moral component of personality, containing the conscience and the ego-ideal.
    8. b) Displacement: The anger is shifted from the threatening target (the boss) to a safer, less threatening target (the dog).
    9. d) Free will and the drive for self-actualization: Humanistic psychology focuses on the positive aspects of human nature and the innate drive to achieve one's full potential.
    10. a) Denial: Denial is the outright refusal to accept or acknowledge a painful reality.

    Chapter Summary (Key Takeaways)

    • Personality is a person's unique and stable pattern of thinking, feeling, and acting.
    • Freud's psychoanalytic theory proposes a conflict between the pleasure-seeking Id, the reality-based Ego, and the moralistic Superego.
    • Defence mechanisms are unconscious strategies used by the Ego to protect against anxiety. Key examples include Repression, Denial, Projection, Rationalization, and Sublimation.
    • Humanistic theories (Maslow, Rogers) focus on positive growth and self-actualization.
    • Trait theory describes personality in terms of stable traits, with the "Big Five" (OCEAN) model being the most prominent.
    • Understanding these theories provides essential tools for a social worker to comprehend client behavior.
