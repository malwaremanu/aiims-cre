## **Detailed Theory / Concepts**

### **Part 1: Analogies**

An analogy is a form of reasoning where a relationship between two items is established, and the same relationship is then used to find a missing item in another pair. The goal is to identify the underlying logic connecting the first pair.

The standard format is **A : B :: C : ?**
This is read as: "A is to B as C is to [what?]"

#### **Types of Word-Based Analogies**

Understanding these common relationships is the key to solving analogy problems.

| **Relationship Type** | **Example** | **Explanation** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cause and Effect** | Virus : Disease | A virus is the cause of a disease. |
| **Part to Whole** | Chapter : Book | A chapter is a part of a whole book. |
| **Worker and Tool** | Surgeon : Scalpel | A surgeon uses a scalpel as their tool. |
| **Object and Category** | Whale : Mammal | A whale belongs to the mammal category. |
| **Synonyms/Antonyms** | Abundant : Plentiful | These words are synonyms (have the same meaning). |
| **Intensity** | Warm : Hot | Hot is a higher intensity of warm. |
| **Symbol and Meaning** | Dove : Peace | A dove is a symbol of peace. |
| **Country and Capital** | Japan : Tokyo | Tokyo is the capital of Japan. |
| **Product and Raw Material**| Cloth : Fibre | Cloth is made from fibre. |

#### **How to Solve Analogy Questions**
1.  **Decode the Relationship:** First, precisely identify the relationship between the first pair (A and B). Be specific. "Surgeon : Scalpel" isn't just about medicine; it's about a professional and their specific tool.
2.  **State the Relationship:** Form a clear sentence. "A surgeon uses a scalpel."
3.  **Apply to the Second Pair:** Apply the same sentence structure to the second item (C). "A sculptor uses a [what?]."
4.  **Find the Match:** The answer would be "Chisel."

#### **Number and Letter Analogies**
*   **Number Analogies:** These are based on mathematical operations.
    *   **Example:** `8 : 64 :: 9 : ?`
    *   **Logic:** The relationship is `x : x²`. So, 8² is 64.
    *   **Answer:** 9² is 81.
*   **Letter Analogies:** These are based on the position or sequence of letters in the alphabet.
    *   **Example:** `ACE : GIK :: BDF : ?`
    *   **Logic:** Each letter in the first group is skipped by one (`A`_`C`_`E`). The second group starts after skipping one letter from the end of the first group (`E`_`G`). The same pattern (`B`_`D`_`F`) applies.
    *   **Answer:** `HJL` (skip one letter after F, which is G, start with H).

### **Part 2: Classification (Odd One Out)**

Classification questions present a group of four or five items. All but one of these items are similar to one another in some way. Your task is to identify the item that does not fit the group.

#### **Types of Classification**
*   **Word Classification:** Based on a common property of words (e.g., meaning, category).
    *   **Example:** `A. Lion`, `B. Tiger`, `C. Leopard`, `D. Cow`
    *   **Logic:** Lion, Tiger, and Leopard are carnivores and wild cats. A cow is a domestic herbivore.
    *   **Answer:** Cow.
*   **Number Classification:** Based on a common mathematical property.
    *   **Example:** `A. 25`, `B. 49`, `C. 81`, `D. 65`
    *   **Logic:** 25 (5²), 49 (7²), and 81 (9²) are perfect squares. 65 is not.
    *   **Answer:** 65.
*   **Letter Classification:** Based on patterns in letter groups (vowels, consonants, sequence).
    *   **Example:** `A. IO`, `B. EA`, `C. OU`, `D. UP`
    *   **Logic:** IO, EA, and OU are pairs of vowels. UP contains a consonant.
    *   **Answer:** UP.

## **AIIMS CRE-Oriented Tips & Tricks**

*   **Build General Knowledge:** Word analogies often depend on your vocabulary and general awareness. Regularly read and learn about countries, capitals, currencies, and scientific facts.
*   **Master the Alphabet:** For letter-based questions, memorize the numerical position of each letter (A=1, B=2...) and its reverse position (Z=1, Y=2...). The "EJOTY" (5, 10, 15, 20, 25) and "V" (22) trick can help you quickly locate letter positions.
*   **Sharpen Math Skills:** For number-based questions, be quick with squares, cubes, prime numbers, and basic arithmetic operations.
*   **Think Broadly, then Narrowly:** In classification, first look for a very obvious similarity. If none exists, look for more subtle connections. For example, all might be animals, but three might be mammals while one is a reptile.
*   **Eliminate Options:** In analogies, if you are unsure, try to eliminate options that have a clearly incorrect relationship.

## **Chapter Summary (Key Takeaways)**

*   **Analogies** test your ability to identify and replicate relationships between pairs of items. Success depends on recognizing the specific type of connection (e.g., cause-effect, part-whole).
*   **Classification** tests your ability to find the "odd one out" from a group by identifying a common property that all but one of the items share.
*   These questions can be based on words, numbers, or letters, each requiring a different set of background knowledge (vocabulary, math skills, alphabet positions).
*   A systematic approach—identifying the core relationship for analogies and the common property for classification—is crucial.
*   Practice is key to improving both speed and accuracy for this important section of the reasoning paper.
