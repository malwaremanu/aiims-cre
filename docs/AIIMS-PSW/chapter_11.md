## **Detailed Theory / Concepts**

### **What is Reading Comprehension?**

Reading comprehension is not just about understanding the words on the page; it's about understanding the ideas behind them. It involves grasping the main point, seeing how the arguments are structured, making logical inferences, and understanding the author's tone and purpose.

### **Active Reading Strategies**

Passive reading (simply letting your eyes move over the words) is not effective for RC. Active reading means engaging with the text.

1.  **The Question-First Approach:** Before reading the passage, take 30 seconds to read the *questions* (not the options). This tells your brain what specific information to look for, turning your reading into a targeted search mission.
2.  **Skimming:** This is a fast reading technique to get a general overview of the passage. Quickly read the first and last paragraphs, and the first sentence of every other paragraph. This helps you identify the main idea or theme.
3.  **Scanning:** Use this when you are looking for a specific piece of information, like a name, date, or keyword that you identified from the questions. Let your eyes drift quickly over the text until you find the target word or phrase.
4.  **Underlining/Annotating:** As you read, underline key points, transition words (like *however*, *therefore*, *in contrast*), and any sentences that seem to directly answer the questions you read earlier.

### **Types of Comprehension Questions**

Understanding the different types of questions helps you know what to look for.

| **Question Type** | **What it Asks** | **How to Answer** |
| : | : | : |
| **1. Main Idea / Theme** | "What is the primary purpose of the passage?" or "The passage is mainly about..." | The answer is a summary of the entire passage, often found in the introductory or concluding sentences. |
| **2. Direct / Fact-Based** | "According to the passage, why...?" or "The author states that..." | The answer is explicitly written in the text. Your job is to locate it. |
| **3. Inference** | "What can be inferred from the passage?" or "The passage implies that..." | The answer is not stated directly. You must draw a logical conclusion based on the evidence and statements provided in the text. |
| **4. Vocabulary-in-Context**| "What is the meaning of the word 'X' as used in the passage?" | Substitute the given options for the word in the sentence. The correct answer will fit the context without changing the sentence's meaning. |
| **5. Tone / Attitude** | "What is the author's tone?" or "The author's attitude towards 'X' is..." | Look at the author's choice of words (adjectives and adverbs). Are they positive, negative, or neutral? (e.g., *critical, supportive, objective, biased*). |

### **A Step-by-Step Method for RC Passages**

1.  **Step 1: Read the Questions (30 seconds):** Quickly read the questions to know what you're looking for.
2.  **Step 2: Skim the Passage (1 minute):** Read the first and last paragraphs to find the main idea.
3.  **Step 3: Read the Passage in Detail (2-3 minutes):** Read the full passage, underlining keywords and phrases related to the questions.
4.  **Step 4: Answer the Questions (2-3 minutes):**
    *   Tackle direct questions first.
    *   For inference questions, make sure your conclusion is supported by evidence from the text.
    *   Eliminate wrong answers. Incorrect options are often:
        *   **Too Extreme:** Using words like *always, never, all, none*.
        *   **Out of Scope:** Containing information not mentioned in the passage.
        *   **Contradictory:** Stating the opposite of what the passage says.


## **Practice Passage**

**Directions:** Read the passage carefully and answer the questions that follow.

The concept of resilience is a cornerstone of modern mental health practice. It refers to the ability of an individual to "bounce back" from adversity, trauma, or significant stress. For many years, the focus in psychology was on pathology—what is wrong with an individual. However, the rise of positive psychology has shifted the focus towards understanding and fostering strengths. Resilience is not an innate, unchangeable trait that people either have or do not have; rather, it is a dynamic process that can be learned and developed.

Several factors contribute to resilience. One of the most critical is the presence of supportive relationships, both within the family and in the broader community. A person with a strong social support network is better equipped to handle life's challenges. Another key factor is a positive and realistic outlook. This does not mean ignoring problems, but rather facing them with the belief that one can overcome them. Cognitive-behavioral techniques are often employed to help individuals reframe negative thought patterns that undermine resilience. Finally, a sense of purpose or meaning in life provides a powerful anchor during turbulent times. For a psychiatric social worker, the goal is not merely to alleviate symptoms, but to actively cultivate these resilience factors in their clients, empowering them to lead fulfilling lives despite their challenges.

1.  **b) The definition, components, and cultivation of resilience:** The entire passage is structured around defining resilience, explaining the factors that contribute to it, and mentioning how it can be fostered.
2.  **c) A fixed, unchangeable personality trait:** The passage explicitly states the opposite: "Resilience is not an innate, unchangeable trait... it is a dynamic process that can be learned and developed."
3.  **b) They focused more on weaknesses than on strengths:** The passage states, "For many years, the focus in psychology was on pathology—what is wrong with an individual. However, the rise of positive psychology has shifted the focus towards understanding and fostering strengths." This implies the older approach was focused on the negative (pathology/weaknesses).
4.  **c) To grow or develop:** In this context, "cultivate" means to actively encourage the growth and development of resilience factors in clients, much like a gardener cultivates plants.
5.  **c) Informative and optimistic:** The passage provides information about resilience in a straightforward manner (informative) and presents it as a learnable skill that can empower people, which is a hopeful and positive perspective (optimistic).

## **Chapter Summary (Key Takeaways)**

*   Reading Comprehension tests your ability to analyze written information, not just read it.
*   Adopt active reading strategies like the "Question-First Approach" to make your reading focused and efficient.
*   Learn to identify the different types of questions (Main Idea, Inference, Direct, etc.) to know exactly what you are looking for.
*   A systematic, step-by-step approach will help you manage your time effectively and answer questions with confidence.
*   The process of elimination is a powerful tool to discard incorrect answers and increase your probability of selecting the right one.
